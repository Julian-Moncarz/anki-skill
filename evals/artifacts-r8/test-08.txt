TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are both transport-layer protocols, but they differ in key ways:

**TCP — Connection-oriented, reliable**
- Establishes a connection via a **three-way handshake** (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) before sending data
- **Guarantees delivery** — retransmits lost packets
- **Guarantees ordering** — reassembles packets in the correct sequence
- **Flow control & congestion control** — adjusts sending rate to avoid overwhelming the receiver or network
- Higher overhead due to all of the above
- Used for: HTTP/HTTPS, SSH, email (SMTP), file transfer (FTP)

**UDP — Connectionless, unreliable (by design)**
- No handshake — just sends packets (datagrams)
- **No delivery guarantee** — lost packets are not retransmitted
- **No ordering guarantee** — packets may arrive out of order
- **No flow/congestion control**
- Very low overhead, lower latency
- Used for: DNS lookups, video/audio streaming, online gaming, VoIP

**In short:** TCP trades speed for reliability; UDP trades reliability for speed. Choose TCP when you need every byte to arrive correctly, and UDP when speed matters more than occasional data loss.
